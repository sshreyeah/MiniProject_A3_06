Why these scalers?
Rationale for scaler selection
StandardScaler
Used for avg_weekly_usage_hours and tenure_months — both have near-symmetric distributions (skew ≈ 0), making Z-score normalization appropriate. Centers data at mean=0, std=1.
RobustScaler
Used for support_tickets and payment_failures — these can have extreme outlier values (e.g., 8 tickets, 5 failures). Uses median and IQR instead of mean/std, so outliers don't skew the scale.

Patterns & Trends

Key Findings &
Conclusions

Summary of all patterns identified across univariate, bivariate, and multivariate analyses — with overall trend and business implications.

1
Usage pattern drives churn
Customers with low usage (<8h/week) churn at 65.95% — the highest of any segment. High usage customers churn at only 54.30%. Engagement directly predicts retention.
2
Tenure trend
Short-tenure customers (0-12 months) show higher churn. Long-term customers are more loyal — indicating a loyalty cliff in early months that, if survived, improves retention odds.
3
Plan type has no impact
Basic (57.85%), Premium (58.05%), Standard (56.06%) — all nearly identical. Subscription tier is not a churn predictor. Pricing interventions alone won't solve retention.
4
Payment failures signal risk
Churn probability climbs with payment failures. Zero failures → ~50% churn; five failures → ~65% churn. Payment friction is an actionable early warning indicator.
5
Login recency predicts churn
Customers inactive for 46-60 days show markedly higher churn rates (~68%). Last login date is a near real-time behavioral signal for intervention campaigns.
6
Support tickets correlate
Churned customers file more support tickets across all plan types (avg 4.13-4.32 vs 3.37-3.51 for retained). High ticket volume may indicate dissatisfaction preceding churn.
Overall trend
Conclusion
Customer churn in this dataset is strongly influenced by behavioral engagement metrics — specifically usage frequency and login recency — rather than by subscription type or pricing tier. Customers with low activity and shorter subscription duration are significantly more likely to leave.

The data suggests a three-signal early warning system: (1) login inactivity > 30 days, (2) avg weekly usage < 8h, (3) payment failures ≥ 3. Any customer triggering two or more signals is a prime retention target.

Since plan type has negligible impact on churn rate (~57% across all tiers), retention strategy should focus on re-engagement campaigns, usage nudges, and proactive payment failure resolution rather than plan upselling.
